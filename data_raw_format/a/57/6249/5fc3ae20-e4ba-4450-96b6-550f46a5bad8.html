    I am currently the Academic Chair for the Internetworking and Network Security Major.
    I teach a variety of network design units at both undergraduate and postgraduate levels. While I have a research interest in many aspects of networking and security my focus is on the use HF as a medium for wireless sensor networks.

    Current projects

    HF Wireless Sensor Networks

    I am involved in a long term project investigating the use of HF (High Frequency) wireless communications to support wireless sensor networks over very large distances with low power and no supporting infrastructure.

    HF receive antenna being installed at Murdoch University

    Despite its name, HF uses much lower frequencies (3-30MHz) than the wireless devices most of us use on a daily basis.  What makes this frequency range unique is that the ionosphere, at an altitude of several several hundred kilometers, reflects some of the signal back to earth.  This allows communications over thousands of kilometers without the need for elevated towers or satellites to relay the signals.

    The challenge with HF is predicting when communications will be possible because the ionosphere’s behaviour is strongly influenced by solar radiation.  Day, night, seasons, latitude and even sun spots have a profound effect on HF propagation.  Some of these events are cyclical while others are unpredictable.  Although we can model the ionosphere this is not trivial for a low power sensor with limited battery and CPU capacity.

    Much of the research involves the trade-off between using limited resources to accurately determine when to transmit verses unnecessary, power consuming, transmissions that are unlikely to be received.

    Field Trip and Experiment

    In January 2017 I embarked on a field trip to Yellowdine (West of Kalgoorlie), Baladonia, Eucla and Ceduna (SA).  At each location a battery powered transmitter and 6 metre fibreglass antenna were installed in remote bushland (see picture).  A receiver at Murdoch University was used to detect transmissions over the six days the experiment  was run.

    Wireless Sensor - Baladonia

    The locations were selected so as to provide a range of reflection angles, progressive exposure to solar radiation across the day and their proximity to GPS ground stations owned by the Government agency Geoscience Australia.  Radio signals between a GPS satellite and ground receiver pass through the ionosphere. We intend to use GPS signal strength and timing data from Geoscience Australia to examine the feasibility of using a GPS contained within a wireless sensor, to measure the ionosphere in real-time to allow transmissions to be scheduled at a time that maximises their chance of being received.
