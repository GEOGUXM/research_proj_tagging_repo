William Nelson ’65 has been active as a legal historian since the publication of his first history article in the New York University Annual Survey of American Law in 1966. Over the intervening years, Nelson has published two prize-winning books, eleven other books, and numerous articles in leading law reviews and history journals. Nelson has been a mainstay of NYU School of Law’s first-year teaching faculty since he returned to the Law School as a professor in 1979. He has taught either Contracts, Property, Torts, or Professional Responsibility to a large section of students in each of the past 34 years. In the upper-year curriculum, Nelson has focused on training future law teachers, especially in his seminar on legal scholarship and legal history. Nelson’s first love during the past decade has been research for a monograph that focuses on the history of the common law in colonial America.

Featured Work

“Political Decision Making by Informed Juries”

Our legal system wants juries to exercise political power. In the William & Mary Law Review, William Nelson argues that it is not whether juries should engage in political choice but how they should engage in it that matters.

Courses

  • Colloquium on Legal and Constitutional History

    This Colloquium examines the work of leading scholars writing on legal and constitutional history, mainly books and articles in draft being written by current scholars but occasionally classic published works from the past. The topics that will be addressed are wide-ranging; last year they included chapters from a forthcoming book on the history of the Warren Court, a book on medieval Islamic legal history, and a work on the imposition of English law in the British colony of Gold Coast. Students receive credit either by submitting a required number of brief response papers or by writing a substantial research paper for which they may receive additional writing credit. Students writing a substantial research paper will be required to present it orally to the group. The colloquium may be elected either for one or both semesters. 2 credits. Consent of instructor is required to register.

  • Great Cases in Supreme Court History Seminar

    This seminar will enable students who select it to write a paper on a leading case decided during the course of the Supreme Court's history. Classes during the first several weeks of the semester will consider why one might want to turn to historical analysis of past cases and some different forms that a turn to history might take. In subsequent classes, all students will be required at some time over the course of the semester to present drafts of the papers they are writing.

  • Legal History Colloquium

    This course, taught by faculty members of the School of Law, is designed for students planning academic careers, in particular, as legal historians. Students who take the course will be expected to work on a major research paper intended for publication. Specific topics discussed during the semester are determined by the interests of those participating in the colloquium. 2 credits. Consent of instructors is required to register.

  • Legal Scholarship

    This course is designed principally to introduce prospective law teachers to the various sorts of legal scholarship currently appearing in print and, in appropriate cases, to provide them with an opportunity to write. The course might also prove useful to editors of student publications and to students with an avocational interest in scholarly writing. The course meets once each week in the first semester for two hours. Students taking the course for two credits can select one of several shorter writing projects. Alternatively, students seeking three credits may research and write their own major paper designed for publication. The consent of the instructor is required to register.

  • Professional Responsibility and the Regulation of Lawyers

    What are the ethical obligations of lawyers? The Model Rules of Professional Responsibility elaborate a series of duties to individual clients. The history of the legal profession suggests a different set of duties - toward the development and maintenance of the rule of law. That history also shows that the rule of law is often in tension with the law of rules. This course will examine a series of problems in which fidelity to the rule of law conflicts with the Model Rules and will ask students to think about how, if they were to face such conflicts, they would resolve them. The course satisfies the Professional Responsibility requirement.

  • Property

    A study of the institution of property: property interests in land and in wealth other than land; formation of interests in land; the estate concept; possessory and non possessory interests; concurrent interests; the landlord-tenant relation; the allocation and development of land resources by private arrangement and through community planning devices such as zoning and eminent domain.
