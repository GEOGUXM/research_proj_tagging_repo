About me

Welcome to all who may read my staff page. I have been at UniSA since 2005, and prior to this have been engaged in the higher education sector for a number of years. I have worked in the roles of clinical tutor, sessional tutor, clinical faciltiator and lecturer. I am based within the Bachelor of Nursing (BN) program at City East campus. I currently coordinate ( both primary and associate role) courses in the second year of the undergraduate degree around the acute assessment and management of the adult and care requirements addressing the concerns of chronic illness in the older adult, with an emphasis of having these persons well enough to be as independent as possible. I have had 25 years experience working as a registered, clincal, and clinical nurse consultant, both in public and private hosptials. I am currently engaged with an industry partner where I have the opportunity to observe current practice and integrate this into the curriculum of the courses that I teach. I have a strong interest in medical- surgical nursing, and have clinical experience in specialty areas, recovery and day surgery, accident and emergency and coronary care.

Current research & highlights

I have a strong interest in current work force planning pertaining to the educational preparation of registered nurses and midwives for future beginning graduate practice. This looks at the models of clinical placement, the networks that are present with industry partners, and preparing students to be work ready.,My PhD research pertains to final year undergraduate nursing and midwifery students and their pre-registration paid employment choice, i.e. the part-time work that they choose to support themselves during their nursing studies. The research determined any link between a specific paid employment type ( or no employment at all) and sucessful transition to graduate practice as a registered nurse or midwife is largely arbitary. Rather, this mixed-methods study found that post-registration factors are the major influence on sucessful transition to graduate nurse practice. Specifically, a robust and enduring oreintation at each and every subsequent clinical rotation, appropriate patient allocation with an acuity that can be safely managed by a new graduate, and respectful and meaningful feedback were the key drivers of positive transition experiences.

These courses focus on injury and illness prevention, assessment and management of chronic diseases, and utilise a holistic focus to care when adressing systems issues. I also teach in foundation courses within the first year of the undergraduate nursing degree, and have recently coordinated the final course prior to graduation.
